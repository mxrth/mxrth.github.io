NIST Standardization: Round 3 close to completion

Last week NIST announced that it close to announcing the “winners” of its Post Quantum Cryptography “competition” 1, after promising to have an announcement “by the end of March”.

It stresses that this is “not due to technical considerations but is due to some legal and procedural steps” which is most certainly a nod to recent uncertainties after the IDF announced a possible cryptanalytic development that could obliterate the security margin of some finalists (namely the KEMs CRYSTALS-Kyber and Saber and the signature algorithm CRYSTALS-Dilithium).

Some weeks earlier Ward Bullens completely broke Rainbow, one of the Round 3 finalists, which sure doesn’t help with the overall confidence.2

OpenSSH implements PQC

Since OpenSSH 9.0 the key exchange method defaults to a hybrid scheme, that is one combining a “classical” with a “post-quantum” cryptographic primitive. In this case the PQC Streamlined NTRU Prime (a lattice based KEM) is combined with the classical ECDH over Curve25519 (based on elliptic curve cryptography) 3.

With this hybrid approach OpenSSH follows the advice offered by the French ANSSI and German BSI while pointedly ignoring the NSA. This key exchange method enabled in version 8.9 (released roughly a month prior) after being experimentally included in version 8.5.

While Streamlinded NTRU Prime is not a Round 3 Candidate of the NIST competition, it is one of several “alternate candidates” 4.

  1. I put both “winners” and “competition” in quotes because NIST is adamant that this process shouldn’t be viewed as a competition and in particular schemes NIST does not choose to standardize should not be viewed as “losers”. ↩︎

  2. While this attack could be remedied by moving to larger security parameters I have my doubts that NIST will standardize Rainbow any time soon ↩︎

  3. Incidentally both closely associated with Daniel J. Bernstein (djb) ↩︎

  4. Its “cousin” NTRU (wich uses cyclotomic polynomials instead of polynomials of the form $x^p-x-1$, $p$ prime) is one of the Round 3 finalists ↩︎
