Stable Reduction of Curves

  • Jan 24, 2021
  • 2 minutes read

Three years ago in the last year of my bachelor studies, while writing my thesis in algebraic geometry, I decided to take a seminar called “Stable Reduction Of Curves”. It seemed intriguing: prerequesites were basics of scheme theory (i.e. Chapter II and parts of Chapter III of Harthsorne) , the seminar was run by a renowned arithmetic geometer and so far I hadn’t seen many applications of all that wondrous and intimidating machinery that is scheme based algebraic geometry to number theory.

And see, that’s also kind of where the problem with this seminar laid: I hadn’t really seen much algebraic number theory so far. I didn’t even know what quadratic reciprocity was! (And I still don’t really know, if I’m being honest.) So even while I understood “my” small corner of the proof, I never really made sense of the big picture. Now, three years later (and, because I switched fields, with no additional algebraic geometry knowledge at my disposal), I’ll try to understand what exactly we tried to proof there.

The seminar was based on a paper by Artin and Winters, which (re-)proved the following theorem:

Theorem (Stable Reduction of Curves).
Let $C$ be a smooth proper curce of genus $g$ over the field of fractions $K$ of a discrete valuation ring $R$ with algebraically closed residue field $k$. There is a finite field extension $K'$ of $K$ such that $C_{K'}$ has stable reduction over the integral closure $R'$ of $R$ in $K'$.

Even though I knew most of the words, this still read like gobbledigook. Why on earth would anyone find this theorem interesting much less try to prove it? To unwrap this, I’ll zoom out to the very big picture and begin with the question “What even is Arithmetic Geometry?”
